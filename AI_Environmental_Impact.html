How we estimate tokens from cost

Formula: Monthly cost ÷ blended price per million tokens = tokens used.

Blended price uses a 70% input / 30% output split. Input tokens (what you send) are cheaper than output tokens (what Claude responds with). Sonnet: $3/M input, $15/M output → ~$6.50/M blended. Opus: ~$22/M. Haiku: ~$1/M.

Source: Anthropic pricing page. Output tokens cost ~5× more than input — response length has an outsized impact on both cost and energy.

How we calculated CO₂

Step 1 — Tokens to energy: Each token requires GPU compute. We use 0.39 joules per token, measured on NVIDIA H100 GPUs running vLLM with FP8 quantization at batch size 128. Source: llm-tracker.info — Lin 2025.

Step 2 — Facility overhead (Power Usage Effectiveness / PUE): PUE is the ratio of total data center energy to IT equipment energy. A PUE of 1.2 means 20% overhead for cooling, lighting, and power distribution. We multiply raw compute energy × 1.2. Source: arXiv — "How Hungry is AI?" 2025.

Step 3 — Energy to CO₂: Total kWh × 650g CO₂/kWh. This reflects Indiana's grid (AWS Project Rainier's location): 42% coal, 35% gas — one of the dirtiest grids in the US, significantly above the national average of 386g/kWh. Sources: EIA Indiana 2024; lowcarbonpower.org.

Tier 2 — amortized training: Training is a one-time event shared across all queries ever made. We estimate GPT-4-scale training at ~14,000 metric tons CO₂e and divide by 5 billion estimated queries. Source: Towards Data Science 2023.

Tier 3 — embodied hardware: Manufacturing a single A100 GPU emits ~150 kg CO₂e. Amortized over an estimated 100M-query lifespan per GPU. Source: Nature Scientific Reports 2024.

How we calculated water

On-site (Scope 1): Evaporative cooling towers at the data center. AWS self-reports a global Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) of 0.15 liters per kWh in 2024 — a 40% improvement since 2021 and about 12× better than the industry average of 1.8 L/kWh. This is genuinely low: AWS uses air-side economization and direct evaporative cooling optimized for efficiency. Source: Amazon Sustainability Report 2024.

Power plant water (Scope 2): Coal and gas plants require water for steam and cooling. Indiana's fossil-heavy grid uses roughly 2–3 liters per kWh at the power plant. We use 2.5 L/kWh. This upstream figure is about 17× larger than the on-site cooling number — the real water story is in the power grid, not the data center. Source: ScienceDirect 2025; corroborated by EESI (Lawrence Berkeley Lab 2024).

This water evaporates — it's not contaminated, but it leaves the local watershed. The IEA estimates 60% of data center water consumption comes from this indirect upstream use. For context: UC Riverside estimated ~519 mL per 100-word AI prompt. Source: EESI.

How we calculated energy

Token energy: When Claude processes your prompt, graphics processing units (GPUs) draw power. We use 0.39 joules per token — measured directly on NVIDIA H100 hardware running the vLLM serving framework with FP8 quantization (a compression technique that cuts memory use with minimal quality loss) at batch size 128 simultaneous requests. This is ~120× more efficient than early GPT-3 era estimates due to better hardware and software. Source: llm-tracker.info, Lin 2025.

Converting to watt-hours: A joule is a very small energy unit — 3,600 joules = 1 watt-hour. So: (tokens × 0.39) ÷ 3,600 = raw watt-hours of compute.

Facility overhead (PUE 1.2): Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) accounts for all the energy a data center uses beyond the servers themselves — cooling systems, power distribution units, backup generators, networking, and lighting. PUE 1.2 means 20 cents of overhead for every $1 of compute. Raw Wh × 1.2 = total facility energy.

Trainium2 caveat: Anthropic's Project Rainier runs AWS Trainium2 chips, not H100s. AWS claims 30–40% better price-performance for Trainium2, which may mean lower energy per token — but this is not publicly benchmarked, so we use H100 as the closest available proxy. Source: SiliconAngle — Project Rainier.

How we estimated ChatGPT's numbers — and why this is hard

Starting point — your Claude tokens: We calculate how many tokens you use in Claude from your monthly spend (spend ÷ blended price per million tokens). The ChatGPT column answers: if you sent that same volume of tokens through ChatGPT instead, what would the environmental cost be? This is a like-for-like token comparison, not a spend comparison.

The only disclosed figure: Sam Altman stated in June 2025 that a ChatGPT query averages 0.34 Wh. We use this as the energy-per-query baseline. Source: holdfastprojects.com.

Translating to tokens: We assume an average ChatGPT query = ~500 tokens, giving ~2.4 J/token — significantly higher than the H100 figure used for Claude (0.39 J/token), likely because GPT-4-class models are larger. We apply this rate to your Claude token count to estimate equivalent ChatGPT energy.

CO₂: Azure's grid mix is cleaner than Indiana's. We use 400g CO₂/kWh — between the US average (386g) and Indiana (650g). Microsoft reports WUE of 0.30 L/kWh. Source: Earth911 AI carbon footprint guide 2026.

Why these estimates are unreliable: Published estimates for ChatGPT's CO₂ per query range from 0.15g to 68g — a 450× spread. Google is the only major AI company to publish verified per-query data (Gemini: 0.24 Wh, 0.03g CO₂ per query, August 2025). Source: cnaught.com 2026.
